# Business Analysis — AI-Agent Farmer Calling & SMS Data Business (US)

*July 18, 2026 · Builds on the KG2 US competitor research (docs/kg2-us-competitors-research.md) plus a dedicated sweep for direct competitors and the regulatory picture. Sources linked throughout.*

## The idea in one sentence

Use automated AI voice agents and SMS to survey American farmers at scale — on-farm grain stocks, planting and selling intentions, input purchases, sentiment — and sell that data to the people who need it before USDA publishes: grain buyers, processors, exporters, lenders, input manufacturers, and funds. It is KG2's thirty-year-old Australian playbook with the labor cost removed.

## The direct answer: who is competing with you

**Nobody is doing exactly this.** After roughly a dozen search passes across startup databases, YC and AgFunder portfolios, and every named candidate, no US company was found using automated AI voice or SMS outreach to farmers to build sellable agricultural datasets. The competition instead sits in three rings around the idea, and each ring matters differently.

**Ring one — same data, different method.** These are the companies whose *product* overlaps yours even though their *method* doesn't. Farmer Business Network is the closest single threat: its Planting Intentions and Acreage Reports come out about a week before USDA's, are built from voluntary electronic surveys of its 117,000-farm network, and are explicitly pitched to agribusiness — and FBN raised $50M in 2025 partly to push AI through its platform ([FBN report](https://www.fbn.com/direct/resources/planting-intentions-report), [funding](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20250728822024/en/FBN-Expands-AI-Powered-Platform-for-Ag-Commerce-Financing-and-Farm-Intelligence)). S&P Global Commodity Insights and StoneX sell private acreage and production estimates to traders, but survey analysts and elevators rather than farmers. USDA NASS is the free benchmark you'd be arbitraging — quarterly, aggregate, and shrinking (program cuts in August 2025, and the 43-day late-2025 shutdown left traders paying StoneX and S&P for substitutes) ([NASS](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Surveys/Guide_to_NASS_Surveys/Crops_Stocks/index.php), [shutdown](https://wiky.com/2025/10/09/farmers-traders-flying-blind-as-us-shutdown-blocks-key-crop-data/)).

**Ring two — same model, human methods.** Kynetec is the incumbent whose business you'd be undercutting: 30,000+ US/Canada grower panel, syndicated subscriptions, a rapid-poll product (FarmerPulse), 400+ agribusiness clients, and the EPA paying about $1M a year for a data license. Purdue/CME's Ag Economy Barometer owns "farmer sentiment" mindshare with 400 human phone calls a month. Stratus and Aimpoint run smaller versions. None uses AI agents; all could bolt one on — Kynetec licensing an off-the-shelf AI-interview platform is your most realistic fast-follower scenario ([Kynetec FarmerPulse](https://www.kynetec.com/farmerpulse)).

**Ring three — the assets that could be turned against you.** DTN owns 3M+ farmer records with phone numbers and 900,000 geolocated grain bins; Farm Journal owns a 2.1M-record database and a 5,000-farmer SMS panel it uses editorially; Bushel sits on real grain-transaction data inside elevators; Amber Agriculture puts sensors physically inside bins. Each has a piece of your stack monetized toward advertising or software instead of supply intelligence. The India voice-AI cluster (KissanAI, Farm Vaidya, Digital Green) proves the method works with farmers but sells advisory, not data, and not in America ([DTN grain bins](https://www.thepoultrysite.com/news/2019/03/farm-market-id-offers-onfarm-grain-storage-data-to-agribusinesses), [India voice AI](https://www.ifpri.org/blog/generative-ai-powered-voice-technology-in-agricultural-advisory-services-lessons-from-india/)).

The white space is real: the tooling exists (Voicepanel, Outset, Strella have pushed AI-moderated interviews to roughly $8–15 each versus $150–300 human-moderated), the buyers exist, the data gap exists — and no one has combined them for US agriculture.

## The legal reality — this shapes everything

You **cannot legally cold-call US farmer cell phones with an AI voice.** The FCC ruled in February 2024 that AI-generated voices count as "artificial or prerecorded" under the TCPA, so every AI call to a cell phone requires the person's prior express consent — from call one, regardless of whether you're selling anything ([FCC ruling](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-confirms-tcpa-applies-ai-technologies-generate-human-voices)). Violations run $500–$1,500 per call with no cap, TCPA class actions are an industry, and the FCC has already handed out a $6M forfeiture for AI-voice robocalls. Cold SMS is equally closed: carriers block unregistered traffic outright and fine per violation.

But the door is open where it matters. Survey and research calls are not "telemarketing" under the TCPA, so the harsher written-consent and Do-Not-Call rules don't apply to you; a live human, manually dialing, can legally cold-call farmers to recruit them; and once a farmer has opted in — web signup, inbound text, a human recruitment call, a paid-panel enrollment — AI voice and SMS surveys against that panel are lawful with proper disclosure and opt-out handling ([TCPA survey analysis](https://mslawgroup.com/tcpa-requirements-faq/)). This means the business is legally required to be what it should be commercially anyway: a **consent-based paid panel**, recruited once (human or opt-in), then surveyed cheaply and repeatedly by AI. That's exactly how Kynetec and Ag Access are structured — the AI changes your cost per interview roughly 10–20x, not the legal shape of the funnel. Build to written/checkbox consent covering AI calls and texts explicitly, disclose the AI in-call, register the SMS side (A2P 10DLC), and honor any opt-out within ten business days.

## Who pays, and what the money looks like

Four buyer groups, in order of willingness to pay. **Grain buyers and traders** (elevators, crushers, ethanol plants, exporters, funds) pay for anything that predicts supply before USDA — S&P and StoneX subscriptions and the shutdown episode prove it; this is the unserved group, since every existing farmer panel monetizes toward the input side. **Input manufacturers** (seed, chemical, equipment) already pay Kynetec tens to hundreds of thousands per subscription for farmer-reported usage data — a proven but contested market. **Lenders and insurers** pay for farm financial-health and intentions signals. **Government** is slower but real — the EPA's ~$1M/year sole-source license for Kynetec's survey data is the public benchmark that one agency will pay seven figures annually for farmer-reported data.

The unit economics are the whole story. KG2's quarterly omnibus is ~1,000 human calls; Purdue pays for 400 a month. At $8–15 per AI interview, a 2,000-farmer monthly panel costs roughly $20–30k a month to field (plus farmer incentives, say $10–20 per completed survey — $20–40k more). Call it $50–70k/month fully loaded for a survey operation that would cost a human call center $300–600k. If ten grain-market clients pay $50–100k/year each for the syndicated report — modest against S&P subscriptions — the panel pays for itself at a scale no incumbent can match on cost. KG2's second lesson: the same panel is a direct-marketing asset (recruited, opted-in, profiled farmers are exactly who input makers want to reach), which can subsidize the research side the way it has for KG2 for three decades.

## Moats, and how incumbents respond

Honestly: the AI tooling is not a moat — anyone can rent it. The durable assets are (1) the **consented panel itself**, because recruitment is the slow, expensive, trust-bound step everyone must repeat, (2) the **longitudinal data** — month twelve of the same farmers answering the same stocks question is worth far more than month one, and a fast-follower starts at zero history, (3) **statistical credibility** — the first time your number beats or front-runs USDA's, you have a track record no one can copy, and (4) **farmer trust and experience quality**, where KG2's answer (ag-literate callers, seasonal timing) is the bar the AI must clear. The realistic threats, in order: Kynetec bolts AI moderation onto its existing panel (likely within 1–2 years of anyone proving the market); DTN points AI at the 3M farmer records it already owns (structurally positioned, historically ad-focused); FBN deepens its intentions reports (but its farmers are customers, not a stratified panel, and its data is a byproduct). Speed to a credible published number is the defense against all three.

## The wedge

Everything in the research points to the same starting move, which happens to match the original Caller concept: **one crop, one question, one buyer type.** Wheat (or corn) on-farm stocks and selling intentions, in two or three states, published monthly between USDA's quarters, sold to a handful of grain buyers who are currently blind between reports. Recruit the first 500–1,000 farmers with human calls and paid incentives (legal, and it builds the consent base), field the monthly survey by AI voice + SMS, publish free for two or three cycles to build the track record against USDA benchmarks, then paywall. Expand crops and states only after the first number lands. The direct-marketing revenue line waits until the panel is big enough that selling access doesn't poison trust.

## Verdict

The idea passes the three tests that matter: the demand is proven (buyers already pay S&P, StoneX, and Kynetec for weaker or adjacent versions), the supply-side economics just broke open (10–20x cost collapse in interviews), and the direct competitive lane is empty as of July 2026. The two things that kill it are legal carelessness (cold AI calls are a per-call liability from day one — the consent-first funnel is non-negotiable) and credibility failure (a panel that can't stand next to USDA numbers is content, not data). Structured as a consent-based panel with a narrow first product for grain buyers, this is a genuinely open market with a two-year-ish head start before the incumbents can be expected to respond.
