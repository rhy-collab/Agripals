# The US Competitors to KG2 — Research Report

*Researched July 18, 2026 · Five-angle web research, cross-verified · Sources linked throughout*

## What KG2 actually is (the benchmark)

KG2 has spent 30+ years building what it calls Australia's largest independent farmer database — 100,000+ commercial primary producers — and its engine is people on phones: an engagement platform staffed exclusively by agricultural undergraduates and postgraduates who call farmers at the right point in the season. It monetizes that asset three ways: syndicated and custom market research (its AgScan omnibus phone-surveys around 1,000 farmers quarterly), farmer datasets and analytics sold to agribusiness, banks, and government, and direct marketing that lets corporates reach farmers directly. Buyers use it to understand what's on farm — stocks, intentions, sentiment — ahead of official statistics. ([kg2.com.au/about-us](https://kg2.com.au/about-us/), [AgScan](https://kg2.com.au/agscaninfo/))

The headline finding of this research: **no US company runs the full KG2 stack.** Three companies each own a large piece of it, and they are the top three competitors. But the specific lane — recurring phone contact with an identified farmer panel, sold as supply intelligence to the people who buy grain — is essentially vacant in the United States.

## #1 — Kynetec (St. Louis, MO)

**The closest match, and the biggest.** Kynetec is a global agricultural market research firm — US headquarters in St. Louis — that does almost exactly what KG2 does, at roughly ten times the scale. It maintains proprietary farmer panels covering 30,000+ US and Canadian crop growers (200,000+ farmers and agronomists surveyed annually across ~50 countries), pitches itself to farmers as an independent research company that isn't selling anything, pays panelists per completed survey, and collects data through a mix of telephone interviewing (CATI) and web surveys. ([yourkynetec.com](https://yourkynetec.com/), [kynetec.com/farmtrak](https://www.kynetec.com/farmtrak))

Its flagship products are syndicated tracking studies sold on subscription to agribusiness: FarmTrak (crop protection and seed market share, brand performance, spend per acre — the US version draws on ~25,000 farmers), AgroTrak (the US pesticide-usage database that the EPA licenses as its primary national source — a sole-source federal contract worth $3.76M over ~3.5 years, about $1M a year for one data license), MachineryTrak (equipment), EZTrak (retail-to-farm invoice data), and FarmerPulse (quick-turn farmer polling). Clients: 400+ customers in 80+ countries — crop protection manufacturers, seed companies, machinery OEMs, animal health firms, and governments including the EPA and Bayer. It has ~900–1,500 employees, revenue estimated around $100–133M, and is owned by food-and-ag private equity firm Paine Schwartz, which has done seven bolt-on acquisitions and is reportedly approaching an exit. ([EPA contract on USAspending](https://www.usaspending.gov/award/CONT_AWD_68HERH24P0009_6800_-NONE-_-NONE-), [Paine Schwartz announcement](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/paine-schwartz-partners-invests-in-kynetec-a-leader-in-agricultural-market-research-301250981.html))

**How it differs from KG2:** Kynetec's panels measure what farmers *did* — what they planted, sprayed, and paid — and sell it to the companies who market inputs to farmers. It does not sell on-farm grain stocks or selling intentions to grain buyers, and phone is one mode among several rather than the identity of the business. It also has no direct-marketing arm; it guards panel anonymity instead.

## #2 — DTN / Farm Market iD (Bloomington, MN)

**The biggest farmer database in America, pointed at a different target.** DTN — the ag data and weather company owned by Swiss holding company TBG (bought for ~$900M in 2017, revenue estimated ~$285M today) — acquired Farm Market iD in 2021 and with it the largest independent US farmer database: 3M+ farmer and operation profiles covering 95%+ of US farm operations, 900M+ acres mapped to quarter-acre increments, 150+ data points per producer. Most strikingly for the KG2 comparison, it geolocated 900,000+ on-farm grain bins representing about 11.1 billion bushels (~$75B) of storage capacity, tied to specific fields and named farmers — built from satellite imagery and validated against USDA figures. ([dtn.com FarmMarket data](https://www.dtn.com/agriculture/agribusiness/farmmarket-data/), [grain bin dataset](https://www.thepoultrysite.com/news/2019/03/farm-market-id-offers-onfarm-grain-storage-data-to-agribusinesses))

DTN also runs an actual telephone survey of farmers — the DTN/Progressive Farmer Agriculture Confidence Index, ~500 farmers three times a year — and serves grain buyers through a proprietary network collecting 36,000–40,000 cash bids daily from ~4,200 elevators and ethanol plants, plus the ProphetX trading terminal. ([Ag Confidence Index](https://www.dtnpf.com/agriculture/web/ag/news/special-features/ag-confidence-index), [cash bids](https://www.dtn.com/why-watching-daily-grain-bids-is-important/))

**How it differs from KG2:** DTN monetizes its farmer database almost entirely as an advertising and direct-marketing asset (programmatic ad targeting through Progressive Farmer and AgTarget) — the direct-marketing half of KG2's model at enormous scale. Its grain intelligence comes from prices and bids, not from asking farmers. Its bin data is capacity, not what's currently in the bins; its phone survey measures sentiment, not stocks. It owns every asset needed to be the American KG2 and has not assembled them into that product.

## #3 — Farm Journal (Lenexa, KS)

**The farmer audience company.** Farm Journal is the 150-year-old ag media house (AgWeb, Pro Farmer, Top Producer, Machinery Pete) whose commercial core is FarmReach — a claimed 2.1M+ farm records across ~355M acres with 300+ selectable attributes, appended emails and mobile numbers, enriched by first-party behavioral data from 20 million monthly audience interactions. It sells that as direct marketing and audience access to agribusiness: list rental, programmatic targeting, content marketing, events. It also runs the closest thing America has to a standing farmer polling panel: Farm Journal Pulse, ~5,000 farmers answering questions by text message, plus the famous Pro Farmer Midwest Crop Tour (100+ scouts physically sampling 2,000+ fields each August, feeding a paid newsletter at ~$480/yr) and commissioned surveys off the database reaching tens of thousands of farmers by email. ([FarmReach](https://www.agriculture-xprt.com/services/data-and-service-solutions-522269), [Pulse panel](https://www.agweb.com/news/crops/crop-production/join-farm-journal-pulse-national-panel), [Crop Tour methodology](https://www.profarmer.com/crop-tour-methodology))

**How it differs from KG2:** Farm Journal is media-first. The database exists to sell farmer attention to advertisers, not farmer intelligence to commodity buyers; the Pulse is unweighted editorial content rather than a paid data product; and nothing in the stack is phone-based. It matches KG2's database + direct-marketing half and its farmer-polling instinct, without the research-house monetization.

## Worth knowing (didn't make the top three)

**Stratus Ag Research** (Guelph, ON) runs KG2's exact model — proprietary North American farmer database, syndicated subscription studies — but boutique-sized (~11 staff) and deliberately online-only ("No phone calls" is literally their farmer recruiting pitch). **Ag Access** (Cuba, MO) has a 400,000+ member farmer research community and an in-house telephone call floor — the physical assets of a KG2 — but sells fieldwork services to other researchers rather than its own data products. **Beck Ag** (Wayne, NE) has engaged over a million ag professionals in phone-based peer conversations since 1996, proving outbound farmer calling is a durable US business, but monetizes it as sponsored marketing. **Aimpoint Research** sells syndicated farmer psychographics (Farmer of the Future) without a database or panel. On the grain-intelligence side, **S&P Global Commodity Insights** and **StoneX** sell private acreage/production estimates to traders — but they survey analysts and elevators, not farmers — and the **Purdue–CME Ag Economy Barometer** phone-surveys 400 large producers monthly, for sentiment only, funded by CME as content. ([Stratus](https://stratusresearch.com/custom-research/), [Ag Access](https://ag-access.com/about-us), [Beck Ag](https://beckag.com/our-team/), [Purdue methodology](https://ag.purdue.edu/commercialag/ageconomybarometer/survey-methodology/))

## Implications for an AI-voice version of this model in the US

The gap is real and specific. Nobody in the US commercially measures on-farm grain stocks and selling intentions at the farmer level between USDA's quarterly reports — and farmers hold roughly half of US corn stocks on-farm. The incumbents survey elevators (StoneX), fields (Pro Farmer), satellites (S&P/Lanworth), or moods (Purdue, DTN); none runs a recurring identified-farmer panel asking "what's in your bins and when will you sell." The 43-day government shutdown in late 2025 proved willingness to pay: with USDA dark, traders defaulted to StoneX and S&P estimates while the big merchants traded on private origination data, and USDA has since cut survey programs. Meanwhile the economics that made KG2's model labor-bound have broken open — AI-moderated interviews now cost roughly $8–15 versus $150–300 for human-moderated, meaning a 2,000–5,000 farmer monthly calling panel is feasible at a cost no human call-center incumbent can match. ([USDA NASS methodology](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Publications/Methodology_and_Data_Quality/Grain_Stocks/), [shutdown coverage](https://wiky.com/2025/10/09/farmers-traders-flying-blind-as-us-shutdown-blocks-key-crop-data/))

The honest risks: farmer trust and response fatigue (USDA already struggles with response rates, and KG2's answer — ag-degree humans calling at the right time in the season — is precisely the thing an AI voice must match or exceed), the statistical credibility bar (a panel must be stratified well enough to stand next to USDA benchmarks before traders pay), and the incumbency threat that DTN or S&P bolts AI calling onto assets they already own — DTN in particular has the named farmers, their phone numbers, and their geolocated bins already sitting in one database. The wedge KG2's history suggests: start narrow (one crop, one region, one buyer type — exactly as KG2's clients use it for wheat purchasing decisions), sell the report before scaling the panel, and let the direct-marketing revenue subsidize the research calls, since that's the twin engine that has kept KG2 alive for 30 years.
